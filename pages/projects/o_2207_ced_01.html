Karaoke

University of California, Berkeley

Special Thanks to: Chris Myers + Kuan-Ju Wu (instructors)

A table light’s primary function is simply to bring light to a working surface, but it also does way more—by the means of the table light, an individual regains their agency to act, to observe, and to produce in darkness. In other words, the individual regains their freedom to create. However, any rigid material in table lights will be inclined to limit the freedom of the light. The light is either forcibly directed by the structure, or fixed in dimensions. What if the material used in a table light does not limit the light, but instead to support it, to accentuate the light’s quality of freedom?

In Karaoke, the cardboard, by being torn apart by hand, is devoid of rigid structures and obvious support/fixations of the light. The LED light bulbs are similarly composed in a freeform way, with exposed wires alone acting as support. The cardboards flow naturally around the light source, as if it is shattered and reformed by the light. Two additional choices, the neon pink threads and voice activated light, parallels the user to the light. The user yells to have the light reach peak brightness, symbolically as a burst of passion that explodes the rigid cardboard, yet with neon pink threads, a deliberately human act, the user holds the cardboards together in a new form. The user/light holds over the fate of the cardboards.
