Deconstruction Ceremony

The Red Dot School

Special Thanks to: Nao Kono + Bryan Ortega-Welch (instructors), Maria Glinskaya + Tomona Hashida + Yuta Nihei (team members)

Across Japan, especially in the countryside, a condition called akiya persists. Translated literally, akiya means “empty house.” It is the condition when an owner has left behind their old house for so long, the house has fallen into disrepair. Reasons for moving away are varied but typical, such as population loss in rural areas and effects of an aging population. Such a condition is troublesome both for the owner and the community: the owner does not wish to destroy a place of memories, while the community sees an empty house as a potential hazard. The Deconstruction Ceremony (a play on the traditional groundbreaking ceremony) is resolution to the owner-community relationship through a three day long ceremony.

Within this group work, I am specifically responsible for designing the guidelines to a “Composition Ritual.” In a mock performance of this ritual, I communicated the view that “deconstruction” is not only the instance when the house is torn down, but rather a continuous process throughout a house’s life (ex. the energy systems that passes through a house, the exchange of hands of furnitures and daily items, community assistance with repairs and remodels, the circulation of people and guests). I acted as a master of ceremony, guiding participants of this ritual (owner of the house and community members) through actions that make them realize how a house is in fact an open system, constantly constructed and deconstructed by their communities.

This work also resulted in a manual similar to the instructional arts of Yoko Ono and Sol LeWitt. The instructions are specific in action, but flexible in interpretation, meant to reflect the owner and community’s individual-and-different mental images of the house. For example, one of the instructions asks the participants to arrange “tiresome” items. Although the way to arrange these items as explicitly stated, the participant would interpret what it means to be “tiresome” very differently. A tool for labor for one might be a tool of hobby for another.
